## Preface

I first came to the Gospel of Thomas out of curiosity.

Growing up in an evangelical Christian environment, I was often told to avoid books like this. The Gospel of Thomas was grouped together with other non-canonical writings and labeled as false, dangerous, or misleading. The assumption was simple: if it did not make its way into the New Testament, it had little to offer the sincere seeker of God.

As the years passed, my perspective softened. I began to realize that the story of early Christianity was far more diverse and complex than I had imagined. The first centuries after Jesus were filled with communities spread across the Mediterranean world, each wrestling with the meaning of his life and teachings. Some emphasized faith, others practice. Some focused on church structure, others on mystical experience. Together they formed a rich and varied tapestry of spiritual exploration.

The Gospel of Thomas emerged from one of those communities.

Discovered in 1945 among the Nag Hammadi manuscripts in Egypt, the text consists of 114 sayings attributed to Jesus. Unlike the canonical gospels, it contains no birth narrative, miracles, crucifixion account, or resurrection story. Instead, it presents itself as a collection of teachings, riddles, parables, and paradoxes. Many of its sayings echo material found in Matthew, Mark, and Luke, while others are unique to Thomas.

Scholars continue to debate its origins. Some suggest portions of the text may preserve very early traditions, perhaps dating to the first century. Others believe it took shape later, during the second century, among Christians influenced by emerging mystical and gnostic currents. The truth is that we simply do not know with certainty. What we do know is that generations of seekers found these sayings worthy of preserving, copying, and contemplating.

The word *gnostic* often evokes suspicion in Christian circles. Yet at its root, the Greek word *gnosis* simply means "knowledge"—not merely intellectual knowledge, but direct insight gained through experience. While some ancient gnostic movements developed elaborate cosmologies and theological systems, the Gospel of Thomas often feels far more immediate. Again and again, it points inward. It invites the reader to awaken, to see, to know themselves, and to discover the kingdom that is already present.

That emphasis is one reason this text resonates so naturally with contemplative spirituality.

Throughout these sayings, Jesus speaks of seeing with new eyes. He speaks of finding what is hidden in plain sight, of becoming whole, of reconciling divisions, and of awakening to a deeper reality already woven through ordinary life. The kingdom is not presented primarily as a distant destination, but as a present reality awaiting recognition. Whether one agrees with every saying or not, the text repeatedly calls the reader toward greater awareness, deeper presence, and inner transformation.

This devotional is not intended to be a scholarly commentary. There are many excellent books available for those interested in the history, authorship, and theological debates surrounding the Gospel of Thomas. My purpose is much simpler.

I wanted to sit with these sayings slowly.

I wanted to listen to them as one might listen to a poem, a parable, or a koan. I wanted to explore how they might illuminate the inner life and invite us into greater compassion, presence, and openness to Divine Love. Some sayings feel familiar. Others feel puzzling. A few may even seem unsettling. Rather than forcing a single interpretation, I have tried to approach them with curiosity and humility, allowing the mystery to remain where mystery seems appropriate.

Jesus himself was often a disruptive voice within the religious world of his time. His parables challenged assumptions. His teachings overturned expectations. He regularly invited people to look beyond conventional categories and discover a deeper reality beneath them. Whether or not the Gospel of Thomas represents the same stream of Christianity that ultimately became orthodox, it shares something of that disruptive quality. It asks questions. It unsettles certainty. It invites reflection.

You do not need to accept every idea in this book to benefit from it.

Read slowly. Pause often. Sit with the sayings that resonate and allow the difficult ones to remain open. Let the reflections serve not as conclusions but as companions. The goal is not to arrive at perfect understanding. The goal is to become a little more awake.

May these ancient words help you notice the sacred hidden within the ordinary, the kingdom spread out upon the earth, and the quiet presence of Divine Love that has been accompanying you all along.
