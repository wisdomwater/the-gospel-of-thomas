## 55 - Beyond Every Attachment

> *Jesus said, "Whoever does not hate his father and his mother cannot become a disciple to me. And whoever does not hate his brothers and sisters and take up his cross in my way will not be worthy of me."*

These words can sound harsh to modern ears. They seem at odds with the love and compassion that flow through so many of Jesus' teachings. Yet perhaps this saying is not an invitation to reject our families, but an invitation to examine every attachment that keeps us from living fully in truth.

Family shapes us profoundly. Through family we receive language, beliefs, expectations, fears, loyalties, and ways of seeing the world. Much of this is beautiful and life-giving. Yet some of it can unconsciously define us. We may spend years living according to identities we inherited rather than discovering who we truly are.

The spiritual journey often requires a kind of inner freedom. There comes a moment when we must be willing to step beyond the assumptions and expectations that have shaped us. We are invited to listen for a deeper calling—not one that rejects those we love, but one that refuses to let any human relationship become the ultimate source of our identity.

Jesus then speaks of taking up the cross. Before it became a religious symbol, the cross represented surrender, sacrifice, and the willingness to walk a difficult path. Each of us carries a cross of some kind: the challenge of remaining faithful to truth, the courage to love when it is costly, the willingness to release what is false so that what is true may emerge.

This path is rarely comfortable. It may require disappointing expectations, leaving behind familiar certainties, or embracing a deeper life that others do not fully understand. Yet every authentic transformation asks something of us. We cannot become who we are meant to be while clinging to every old identity and every inherited script.

Perhaps Jesus is inviting us into a love that is larger than attachment. A love that honors family without being confined by family. A love that values relationships without losing itself within them. A love rooted so deeply in the Divine that it can embrace others freely, without fear or dependency.

Today, consider what shapes your sense of self. What expectations, loyalties, or stories are you carrying? Which of them help you live more fully, and which may be limiting your growth? Gently place them before God. Trust that what is true will remain, and what is unnecessary can be released. The path of transformation is not about losing yourself—it is about finding the deeper self that has been waiting beneath every attachment.
