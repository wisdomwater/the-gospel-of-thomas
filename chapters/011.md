## 11 - Returning to the One

> *Jesus said, "This heaven will pass away, and the one above it will pass away. The dead are not alive, and the living will not die. In the days when you consumed what is dead, you made it what is alive. When you come to dwell in the light, what will you do? On the day when you were one you became two. But when you become two, what will you do?"*

This saying unfolds like a series of mysteries, inviting us beyond easy answers. It begins by reminding us that even the grandest things we can see or imagine are not permanent. Worlds change. Structures pass away. What appears solid today will one day be transformed. The saying gently loosens our grip on what is temporary so that we might discover what endures.

We often spend much of our lives identifying ourselves with passing things—our roles, achievements, failures, possessions, and stories. Yet beneath these changing layers is something deeper. There is a life within us that is not diminished by success or failure, youth or age, gain or loss. The saying points toward a dimension of being that participates in something larger than the constant movement of change.

The image of dwelling in the light is especially striking. Many of us imagine that if we could just find clarity, peace, or spiritual understanding, all our questions would disappear. Yet Jesus asks, "When you come to dwell in the light, what will you do?" It is as though awakening is not the end of the journey but the beginning of a new way of living. The question is not merely whether we can see the light, but whether we can learn to live from it.

The final words speak of oneness and division. Before we learn to separate and categorize, life often appears as a seamless whole. As we grow, we develop distinctions—self and other, success and failure, sacred and ordinary. These distinctions can be useful, yet they can also leave us feeling fragmented and disconnected. We begin to experience ourselves as divided.

The contemplative path often involves healing these inner divisions. We learn to embrace both strength and weakness, certainty and mystery, action and rest. We begin to discover that the opposites we carry may belong together within a larger wholeness. The goal is not to erase differences but to see through them into the deeper unity that holds them all.

Perhaps this saying is inviting us to remember what has never been lost. Beneath the changing forms of life, beneath the many identities we wear, beneath the divisions that shape our experience, there remains a deeper unity. The light is already shining there. The invitation is simply to return, again and again, to that place where all things find their source and their rest.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Lead me into the light*
>
> Exhale: *Make me one within*
