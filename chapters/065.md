## 65 - The Voice We Resist

> *He said, "There was a good man who owned a vineyard. He leased it to tenant farmers so that they might work it and he might collect the produce from them..."*
>
> *"...Then the owner sent his son and said, 'Perhaps they will show respect to my son.' Because the tenants knew that it was he who was the heir to the vineyard, they seized him and killed him. Let him who has ears hear."*

This parable tells the story of a vineyard entrusted to others. Again and again, messengers are sent, and again and again they are rejected. What begins as a story about tenants and servants gradually becomes a mirror, inviting us to examine how we respond to truth when it comes knocking at the door of our lives.

Most of us imagine that if wisdom appeared clearly before us, we would immediately welcome it. Yet the reality is often more complicated. Truth has a way of disrupting our plans. It challenges our assumptions. It asks us to release control. Sometimes the very thing that could bring healing is the thing we most resist because it threatens the familiar patterns we have grown comfortable inhabiting.

The tenants in the story cling to what does not belong to them. They mistake stewardship for ownership. They grasp at control and defend it at all costs. In doing so, they lose sight of the relationship that made the vineyard possible in the first place.

We can find a similar tendency within ourselves. We begin to treat our lives, our accomplishments, our beliefs, and even our spiritual insights as possessions. We cling to them. We protect them. We become unwilling to let fresh understanding enter. When this happens, the heart slowly closes itself to transformation.

The servants and the son may symbolize the many ways life continually invites us toward awakening. A quiet intuition. A difficult conversation. A moment of unexpected beauty. A challenge that reveals a hidden attachment. Again and again, grace approaches us in different forms, asking only to be received. Yet we are free to ignore it, resist it, or welcome it.

The good news hidden within this parable is that the invitation keeps coming. The owner does not abandon the vineyard after the first rejection. The messengers continue to arrive. Divine Love is patient. It seeks us through countless circumstances, always inviting us into a deeper relationship with reality, with ourselves, and with God.

Today, consider what message life may be bringing to you. Is there a truth you have been avoiding? A gentle invitation you have postponed? A deeper calling you have hesitated to trust? Listen carefully. The voice of wisdom often arrives quietly. Blessed are those who recognize it and make room for it to enter.
