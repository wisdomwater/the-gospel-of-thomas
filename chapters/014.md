## 14 - The Heart Behind the Practice

> *Jesus said to them, "If you fast, you will give rise to sin for yourselves; and if you pray, you will be condemned; and if you give alms, you will do harm to your spirits. When you go into any land and walk about in the districts, if they receive you, eat what they will set before you, and heal the sick among them. For what goes into your mouth will not defile you, but that which issues from your mouth - it is that which will defile you."*

At first glance, this saying can sound startling. Fasting, prayer, and generosity are often considered noble spiritual practices. Yet Jesus speaks as though even these good things can become obstacles. Perhaps the invitation is not to reject the practices themselves, but to look more deeply at the spirit in which they are performed.

Any practice can become a disguise for the ego. We can fast in order to feel superior. We can pray in order to appear spiritual. We can give generously while secretly craving recognition. The outward action may look admirable, but the inner posture tells a different story. What nourishes the soul is not the appearance of holiness but the sincerity of the heart.

Jesus then shifts the focus toward something beautifully simple. Travel lightly. Receive hospitality. Eat what is given. Heal those who are suffering. Instead of becoming preoccupied with spiritual performance, participate in life with openness, gratitude, and compassion. The sacred is found not only in rituals but in ordinary acts of presence and care.

There is a freedom in this teaching. So much energy can be spent worrying about whether we are doing spirituality correctly. Yet Jesus seems to point toward a life that is less anxious and more authentic. Rather than constantly examining external rules, we are invited to examine the quality of our love, our kindness, and our integrity.

The saying concludes by turning our attention inward. What enters the mouth is less important than what emerges from the heart. Words reveal what is happening beneath the surface. Speech can carry healing or harm, blessing or bitterness, truth or deception. What flows from us often reveals what has been growing within us all along.

Perhaps the invitation today is to release any need to perform spirituality and instead cultivate sincerity. Let your practices deepen your compassion. Let your prayers soften your heart. Let your generosity arise from love. The sacred is not impressed by appearances. It is encountered most deeply in a heart that is honest, open, and willing to love.
