## 13 - Beyond Words

> *Jesus said to his disciples, "Compare me to someone and tell me whom I am like." Simon Peter said to him, "You are like a righteous angel." Matthew said to him, "You are like a wise philosopher." Thomas said to him, "Master, my mouth is wholly incapable of saying whom you are like." Jesus said, "I am not your master. Because you have drunk, you have become intoxicated from the bubbling spring which I have measured out." And he took him and withdrew and told him three things. When Thomas returned to his companions, they asked him, "What did Jesus say to you?" Thomas said to them, "If I tell you one of the things which he told me, you will pick up stones and throw them at me; a fire will come out of the stones and burn you up."*

This saying begins with a simple question: "Whom am I like?" The disciples respond as we often do when faced with mystery. They reach for comparisons, categories, and familiar images. An angel. A philosopher. We naturally try to understand what is unfamiliar by relating it to what we already know.

Yet Thomas responds differently. He admits that he cannot adequately express what he sees. His answer is not certainty but humility. There are realities so deep, so alive, that words cannot fully contain them. Sometimes wisdom begins not with speaking but with recognizing the limits of what can be said.

Most of us spend much of our lives trying to define and explain. We seek clear answers and tidy conclusions. Yet some of the most transformative experiences resist description. Love cannot be reduced to a definition. Beauty cannot be fully captured by analysis. The presence of the sacred often exceeds every concept we create about it.

Jesus responds with an image of drinking from a living spring. The goal is not merely to understand truth but to experience it. A thirsty person can study water endlessly, but eventually they must drink. Spiritual awakening is not simply the accumulation of ideas. It is participation. It is allowing the living water of awareness, love, and presence to become part of who we are.

The mysterious conversation that follows remains hidden from us, and perhaps that is part of the teaching. Not every truth can be handed from one person to another like an object. Some things must be discovered inwardly. Some insights arise only through experience, silence, surrender, and direct encounter. The deepest knowing is often personal, intimate, and difficult to translate into words.

The saying ends with a warning about misunderstanding. New insight can challenge old assumptions, and what awakens one person may disturb another. Yet the invitation is not to become secretive or superior. It is to remain open. To drink deeply from the spring. To allow mystery to remain mystery. And to trust that some truths are better lived than explained.
