## 111 - Living from the Living One

> *Jesus said, "The heavens and the earth will be rolled up in your presence. And the one who lives from the living one will not see death." Does not Jesus say, "Whoever finds himself is superior to the world?"*

This saying opens with an image of startling grandeur. The heavens and the earth themselves are folded away, as though the familiar structures by which we understand reality are no longer sufficient. Everything that appears permanent is revealed to be passing. Everything we once relied upon begins to dissolve.

Most of us spend our lives building our identity upon things that change. We define ourselves by roles, accomplishments, possessions, beliefs, successes, and failures. Yet over time, all of these shift. Seasons change. Circumstances change. Even the stories we tell about ourselves evolve. The heavens and the earth of our personal world are continually being rolled up and remade.

Against this backdrop, Jesus speaks of "the living one." The phrase points toward a life that is deeper than circumstance and more enduring than change. To live from the living one is to discover a source of being that remains present beneath every rise and fall. It is to draw nourishment from the eternal rather than from what is temporary.

The saying then offers a remarkable promise: such a person "will not see death." Contemplatively, this need not be understood merely as a statement about the future. There is a way of living even now that is rooted in what cannot be diminished. When we awaken to the life of the Spirit within us, we begin to recognize a dimension of ourselves that is deeper than fear, deeper than loss, and deeper than the endless cycle of gain and decline.

This understanding is closely connected to the final question: "Whoever finds himself is superior to the world." To find oneself is not to become more important than others. Rather, it is to discover one's true nature beneath the layers of conditioning, expectation, and illusion. The person who has found this deeper self is no longer ruled by the shifting demands of the world because their center of gravity has moved elsewhere.

The world still matters. Its beauty remains beautiful. Its sorrows remain worthy of compassion. Yet it no longer possesses ultimate authority over the heart. The one who has discovered the deeper life can participate fully in the world without becoming imprisoned by it. They can enjoy what comes, release what goes, and remain rooted in a presence that endures.

Today, consider what in your life feels permanent and what is continually changing. Beneath both, there is a deeper current flowing. The invitation of this saying is not to escape the world, but to discover the life that exists before, within, and beyond every changing form. There, in the living one, you may find a peace that nothing can take away.
