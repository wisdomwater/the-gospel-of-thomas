## 63 - The Storehouse and the Soul

> *Jesus said, "There was a rich man who had much money. He said, 'I shall put my money to use so that I may sow, reap, plant, and fill my storehouse with produce, with the result that I shall lack nothing.' Such were his intentions, but that same night he died. Let him who has ears hear."*

This parable begins with a reasonable desire. The rich man is not portrayed as reckless or wasteful. He is planning carefully. He is investing, producing, and preparing for the future. Yet Jesus ends the story abruptly. In the middle of all his plans, the man's life comes to an end.

The shock of the story lies in its reminder that life cannot be secured through accumulation alone. We often believe that if we gather enough—enough money, enough accomplishments, enough possessions, enough certainty—then we will finally be at peace. Yet no storehouse, however full, can guarantee tomorrow.

This saying is not a condemnation of planning or responsible stewardship. Rather, it invites us to examine where we place our trust. The rich man's attention seems consumed by what he can store, manage, and control. There is no mention of wonder, gratitude, relationship, or presence. His future becomes so important that he loses contact with the gift of the present moment.

Many of us live this way without realizing it. We postpone living while preparing to live. We tell ourselves that once a goal is reached, once a problem is solved, once a certain level of security is achieved, then we will rest, love more deeply, or pay attention to what matters most. Yet life continues unfolding while we are making plans for later.

The contemplative path invites a different relationship with time. We still prepare for tomorrow, but we do not sacrifice today to do so. We learn to appreciate what is already here. We discover that true abundance is not measured only by what fills our storehouses, but by what fills our hearts.

The deepest treasures cannot be accumulated. Presence cannot be stored for later. Love cannot be hoarded. Peace cannot be stockpiled. These gifts are experienced only in the living moment. They arrive fresh each day, asking only that we notice them.

Today, consider what storehouses you are building. What are you striving to secure? Then gently ask yourself: What would it mean to live fully now? Not abandoning wisdom or preparation, but refusing to let them overshadow the life that is already being given. The future is uncertain. This moment is not. Let your heart dwell here.
