## 21 - Living with Open Hands

> *Mary said to Jesus, "Whom are your disciples like?" He said, "They are like children who have settled in a field which is not theirs. When the owners of the field come, they will say, 'Let us have back our field.' They (will) undress in their presence in order to let them have back their field and to give it back to them. Therefore I say, if the owner of a house knows that the thief is coming, he will begin his vigil before he comes and will not let him dig through into his house of his domain to carry away his goods. You, then, be on your guard against the world. Arm yourselves with great strength lest the robbers find a way to come to you, for the difficulty which you expect will (surely) materialize. Let there be among you a man of understanding. When the grain ripened, he came quickly with his sickle in his hand and reaped it. Whoever has ears to hear, let him hear."*

This saying gathers several images together: children in a borrowed field, a watchful homeowner, robbers in the night, and grain ready for harvest. At first they seem unrelated, yet they all point toward a single invitation—to live awake, unattached, and attentive to what truly matters.

The image of the children is especially striking. When the owners arrive, the children simply give the field back. They do not cling to it or claim ownership. There is an innocence here, a freedom from possession. Much of our suffering comes from grasping tightly to things that were never ours to keep forever—our roles, our achievements, our plans, even our carefully constructed identities. Life continually reminds us that everything is ultimately a gift.

To live with open hands does not mean we stop caring. It means we stop clinging. We learn to receive each moment gratefully while releasing the illusion that we can permanently possess it. There is a profound peace that comes when we stop trying to hold onto what is meant to flow through our lives.

Jesus then speaks of vigilance. The "robbers" may symbolize anything that steals our awareness and pulls us away from our deepest center. Fear, resentment, distraction, endless busyness, and unconscious habits can quietly enter the house of the soul. We do not guard against them through anxiety, but through presence. A watchful heart notices when it is drifting and gently returns to what is real.

The final image is one of harvest. There are seasons in every spiritual life when something ripens within us. Wisdom matures. Compassion deepens. Old lessons finally bear fruit. The person of understanding recognizes these moments and responds. They do not postpone the work of transformation for another day. They gather the fruit when it is ready.

"Whoever has ears to hear, let him hear." The saying closes not with an explanation but with an invitation. Life is constantly speaking. Every joy, every loss, every ending, and every beginning carries a lesson. The question is not whether wisdom is present. The question is whether we are awake enough to receive it.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Teach me to be awake*
>
> Exhale: *And live with open hands*
