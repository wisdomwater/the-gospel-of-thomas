## 64 - The Invitation Before You

> *Jesus said, "A man had received visitors. And when he had prepared the dinner, he sent his servant to invite the guests..."*
>
> *...The servant returned and said to his master, 'Those whom you invited to the dinner have asked to be excused.' The master said to his servant, 'Go outside to the streets and bring back those whom you happen to meet, so that they may dine.' Businessmen and merchants will not enter the places of my father."*

This parable begins with a feast already prepared. The table is set. The invitation has been extended. Nothing more is required except a response. Yet one by one, the invited guests decline. Each has a reason. Each excuse sounds reasonable. Business, property, obligations, and responsibilities all take precedence over the invitation before them.

There is something deeply human in this. Most of us do not reject the sacred outright. We simply postpone it. We tell ourselves we will pay attention later, when life is less busy, when work settles down, when the next project is complete, when the responsibilities ease. Yet the list of reasons never truly ends. One commitment gives way to another, and the invitation remains unanswered.

The feast in this parable may represent the Kingdom itself—the living experience of God's presence, available here and now. It is not something earned after all our work is finished. It is something offered in the midst of ordinary life. The tragedy is not that the guests are wicked. The tragedy is that they are too occupied to recognize what is being offered.

The master's response is beautiful. The invitation widens. Those found in the streets are welcomed to the table. The Kingdom is not reserved for the successful, the accomplished, or the important. It remains open to anyone willing to receive it. Those who know they are hungry often recognize the feast more quickly than those who believe they have everything they need.

The final statement about businessmen and merchants may point beyond any particular profession. Commerce becomes a symbol for a way of living in which everything is measured, calculated, bought, sold, and evaluated according to usefulness. The Kingdom cannot be approached in this way. It is not a transaction. It cannot be purchased, negotiated, or earned. It can only be received.

Perhaps the question hidden within this parable is simple: What invitations are you missing because you are preoccupied elsewhere? What moments of beauty, silence, connection, and presence are passing unnoticed while your attention remains consumed by the next task? The feast may be closer than you think.

Today, pause for a moment. Set aside your calculations and concerns. Listen for the invitation that has already been extended. The table is prepared. The door is open. The only question is whether you are willing to come.
