## 28 - The Holy Thirst

> *Jesus said, "I took my place in the midst of the world, and I appeared to them in flesh. I found all of them intoxicated; I found none of them thirsty. And my soul became afflicted for the sons of men, because they are blind in their hearts and do not have sight; for empty they came into the world, and empty too they seek to leave the world. But for the moment they are intoxicated. When they shake off their wine, then they will repent."*

This saying paints a vivid picture of the human condition. Jesus looks upon the world and sees people who are intoxicated. Not intoxicated with wine alone, but with distraction, desire, fear, ambition, worry, and the endless pursuit of things that promise fulfillment yet never fully satisfy. They move through life in a kind of spiritual sleep, unaware of the deeper hunger hidden beneath their activity.

What is striking is that Jesus says he found no one thirsty. The problem is not that people lack desire. In fact, much of life is driven by desire. We long for success, security, affection, recognition, comfort, and certainty. Yet these longings often conceal a deeper thirst—one that cannot be quenched by achievement or possession.

There are seasons when we, too, live as though we are intoxicated. We rush from task to task. We fill every silence. We seek relief from discomfort through distraction. We become so occupied with the surface of life that we forget to ask what our hearts truly need. The deeper thirst remains hidden beneath layers of activity.

Yet Jesus does not speak these words with condemnation. He speaks with sorrow and compassion. His soul is afflicted because he sees people settling for less than the fullness of life available to them. He recognizes their blindness not as a moral failure, but as a condition that keeps them from seeing the beauty and presence that surrounds them.

The saying suggests that awakening begins when the intoxication wears off. There comes a moment when the distractions no longer satisfy, when the familiar pursuits lose their power to numb us. What once seemed sufficient begins to feel empty. Though uncomfortable, this can be a sacred turning point. It is often the moment when genuine spiritual longing finally emerges.

To "repent" in this contemplative sense is not merely to feel regret. It is to awaken. It is to see differently. It is to turn toward what is real. The heart begins seeking living water instead of temporary comforts. The soul remembers its deepest desire and starts moving toward it.

Today, gently ask yourself: What am I truly thirsty for? Beneath the noise of daily life, beneath the endless demands and distractions, what longing remains? Sit quietly with that question. You do not need to force an answer. Simply allow the thirst to reveal itself. For the moment we become aware of our deepest longing may also be the moment we begin finding our way home.
