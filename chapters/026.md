## 26 - Seeing Clearly

> *Jesus said, "You see the mote in your brother's eye, but you do not see the beam in your own eye. When you cast the beam out of your own eye, then you will see clearly to cast the mote from your brother's eye."*

This saying begins with a familiar human tendency. We often notice the faults of others with remarkable clarity. Their impatience, pride, selfishness, or inconsistency can seem obvious to us. Yet the habits and blind spots within ourselves often remain hidden from view. Jesus uses an almost humorous image to reveal this truth: a person trying to remove a speck from another's eye while carrying a beam in their own.

There is something in us that naturally looks outward. It feels safer to examine the shortcomings of others than to confront our own. Judgment can become a subtle way of avoiding self-awareness. As long as our attention remains fixed on someone else's flaws, we are spared the discomfort of looking honestly at ourselves.

Yet Jesus does not speak these words to shame us. He offers them as an invitation to clarity. The beam is not a punishment; it is simply something that obscures our vision. We all carry assumptions, wounds, fears, and unconscious patterns that shape how we see the world. Until we become aware of them, we may mistake our perceptions for reality itself.

The contemplative path begins with gentle self-observation. We learn to notice our reactions without immediately defending them. We become curious about the judgments that arise within us. Instead of asking, "What is wrong with that person?" we begin asking, "What is this situation revealing about me?" Such questions open the door to humility and transformation.

Paradoxically, when we stop focusing on the faults of others, we become more capable of helping them. Jesus does not say we should ignore the mote in our brother's eye. Rather, he says that clear vision comes first. A heart that has faced its own struggles develops compassion. It offers guidance without superiority and correction without condemnation.

The more honestly we know ourselves, the more gently we hold others. We recognize that everyone is carrying burdens we cannot fully see. Everyone is learning, stumbling, and growing in their own way. What once provoked judgment begins to evoke understanding.

Today, if you find yourself troubled by the behavior of another, pause for a moment before reacting. Turn your attention inward with kindness and honesty. Ask what this encounter might be teaching you. The beam and the mote are not obstacles to love; they are invitations to deeper awareness. As your vision clears, compassion naturally follows.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Help me see myself clearly*
>
> Exhale: *Help me see others with compassion*
