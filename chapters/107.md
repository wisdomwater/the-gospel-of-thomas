## 107 - The One Who Wanders

> *Jesus said, "The kingdom is like a shepherd who had a hundred sheep. One of them, the largest, went astray. He left the ninety-nine sheep and looked for that one until he found it. When he had gone to such trouble, he said to the sheep, 'I care for you more than the ninety-nine.'"*

This saying draws our attention to the one who has wandered away. While the ninety-nine remain safely gathered, the shepherd's heart is drawn toward the missing sheep. The kingdom, Jesus suggests, is revealed not merely in what is secure and settled, but in the persistent search for what has become lost.

Most of us know what it feels like to wander. Sometimes we drift from our deepest values. Sometimes we lose touch with joy, hope, or trust. At other times, we become separated from parts of ourselves that once felt alive and whole. The wandering may happen gradually, almost unnoticed, until one day we realize we are far from where we intended to be.

Yet the remarkable image in this saying is not the wandering itself. It is the response. The shepherd does not abandon the lost sheep to its fate. Nor does he wait impatiently for it to find its own way home. He goes looking. The movement of the kingdom is toward restoration, toward reunion, toward bringing back into wholeness what has become separated.

Perhaps the lost sheep is not only a person but also a forgotten part of ourselves. We all carry neglected places within us—old wounds, hidden fears, silenced dreams, and tender longings that have been pushed aside. These parts often feel distant from the rest of our lives. Yet the kingdom seeks them too. Divine Love does not concern itself only with the polished and presentable aspects of who we are. It moves toward the forgotten corners with extraordinary care.

The shepherd's words may sound surprising: "I care for you more than the ninety-nine." Perhaps this is not a statement of favoritism but a revelation of attention. The place that is wounded receives healing. The place that is lost receives searching. The place that is struggling receives compassion. Love naturally flows toward what most needs to be restored.

There is great comfort in this image. No matter how far we feel we have wandered, we are never beyond the reach of grace. The sacred does not grow weary of seeking. Again and again, we are invited back—not through shame or force, but through a love that refuses to forget us.

Today, consider what may feel lost within you. Is there a part of your life that longs to be gathered home? Rather than judging it, simply notice it. The kingdom may already be moving toward that very place, carrying an invitation to wholeness, healing, and return.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Seek what is lost*
>
> Exhale: *Bring it home in love*
