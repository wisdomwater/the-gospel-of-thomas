## 104 - The Right Season

> *They said to Jesus, "Come, let us pray today and let us fast." Jesus said, "What is the sin that I have committed, or wherein have I been defeated? But when the bridegroom leaves the bridal chamber, then let them fast and pray."*

There is a surprising freedom in this saying. The disciples offer a spiritual practice that seems noble and familiar—prayer and fasting. Yet Jesus responds by turning their attention elsewhere. Rather than accepting the invitation automatically, he points to the importance of recognizing the moment they are actually living.

How often do we move through our spiritual lives by habit alone? We reach for practices, disciplines, and routines because they are good things. Yet even good things can become disconnected from the living reality before us. A practice meant to awaken the heart can become another item on a list if we lose sight of its purpose.

The image of the bridal chamber speaks of presence, joy, and intimacy. When beloved friends gather for a wedding feast, they are not preoccupied with absence. They are fully present to the celebration unfolding before them. In the same way, there are moments when the sacred is not inviting us into striving, but into participation. Not into effort, but into presence.

This does not diminish prayer or fasting. Rather, it places them within a larger wisdom. There are seasons for seeking and seasons for resting. Seasons for longing and seasons for receiving. Spiritual maturity is not merely knowing what practices to embrace, but discerning what the present moment is asking of us.

Many of us spend energy searching for what is already here. We look for God while standing in the midst of divine presence. We seek reassurance while being held by grace. We strive for connection while the invitation is simply to notice the connection that already exists.

Perhaps today's invitation is to ask: What season am I in right now? Is this a moment to seek, or a moment to receive? Is there something beautiful already present that I have been too busy to notice? The sacred often meets us not only through discipline, but through attentive participation in the gift of the present moment.
