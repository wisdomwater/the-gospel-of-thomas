## 61 - From the Undivided

> *Jesus said, "Two will rest on a bed: the one will die, and the other will live."*
>
> *Salome said, "Who are you, man, that you ... have come up on my couch and eaten from my table?"*
>
> *Jesus said to her, "I am he who exists from the undivided. I was given some of the things of my father."*
>
> *...*
>
> *"Therefore I say, if he is destroyed, he will be filled with light, but if he is divided, he will be filled with darkness."*

This saying is woven from mystery. It speaks of life and death, of division and wholeness, of light and darkness. At first, its images seem difficult to grasp. Yet beneath them lies a theme that appears again and again throughout the Gospel of Thomas: the movement from fragmentation toward unity.

Two people lie in the same bed. One lives, the other dies. Perhaps Jesus is pointing toward something deeper than physical life and death. Two people can share the same circumstances, hear the same words, and walk the same road, yet respond in entirely different ways. One awakens to the deeper life within. The other remains asleep to it.

When Salome asks who he is, Jesus replies, *"I am he who exists from the undivided."* This is a beautiful image. The undivided is the place of wholeness, the source before all separation, the unity beneath every apparent division. It is the deep reality from which all life emerges and to which all life belongs.

Much of human suffering arises from division. We become divided within ourselves. Part of us wants to trust while another part clings to fear. Part of us longs to love while another part remains guarded. We divide the world into opposing camps, divide our lives into sacred and ordinary, divide ourselves from one another and from the Divine. These separations create darkness because they obscure the underlying unity of life.

Jesus then offers a paradox: *"If he is destroyed, he will be filled with light."* Perhaps what must be destroyed is not the true self, but the false self—the collection of fears, defenses, and identities that keep us separate from our deeper nature. As these illusions fall away, the light that was hidden beneath them begins to shine more freely.

The contemplative path is often a journey of integration. We learn to welcome the parts of ourselves we once rejected. We allow love to heal old divisions. We discover that beneath the many fragments of our experience there is a deeper center that remains whole. From that center, life becomes simpler, gentler, and more luminous.

Today, notice any places where you feel divided. Instead of fighting against them, bring them into the light of awareness. Let them rest together in the presence of God. Trust that beneath every fragmentation lies a deeper unity. The light is not something you must create. It is already there, waiting to be uncovered.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Gather me into wholeness*
>
> Exhale: *Fill me with Your light*
