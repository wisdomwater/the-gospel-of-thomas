## 29 - The Treasure Within

> *Jesus said, "If the flesh came into being because of spirit, it is a wonder. But if spirit came into being because of the body, it is a wonder of wonders. Indeed, I am amazed at how this great wealth has made its home in this poverty."*

This saying begins with a mystery and ends with amazement. Rather than offering a clear explanation, Jesus invites us into wonder. He contemplates the relationship between spirit and flesh and then marvels that such great wealth could dwell within what appears to be poverty.

We often judge by appearances. We see the limitations of the body, the fragility of human life, the ordinary routines that fill our days, and we assume that the sacred must be somewhere else. We imagine that divine reality belongs to lofty places, extraordinary experiences, or distant heavens. Yet Jesus points our attention to the astonishing possibility that immense spiritual treasure is already present within ordinary human existence.

Many of us spend our lives focused on what seems lacking. We notice our weaknesses, our fears, our imperfections, and our unfinished growth. We see the cracks in our lives and conclude that we are somehow deficient. Yet this saying invites a different perspective. What if beneath all our perceived poverty there is a hidden richness we have barely begun to discover?

The image of great wealth dwelling within poverty echoes throughout the spiritual journey. The sacred often appears in humble forms: a child in a manger, a seed in the soil, a quiet act of kindness, a moment of silence. Divine life does not wait for perfection before it arrives. It is already present within the imperfect conditions of our humanity.

Perhaps this is why contemplation begins with attention. We learn to stop overlooking the sacred in the ordinary. We begin noticing that every breath is a gift, every moment carries meaning, and every person bears a hidden depth. The more deeply we look, the more we discover that what seemed commonplace is infused with mystery.

Jesus does not merely analyze this reality; he stands in awe of it. There is a lesson here. Spiritual maturity is not only about understanding. It is about wonder. It is about recovering the ability to be astonished by the presence of God in places we once considered ordinary.

Today, take a moment to regard yourself with gentleness. Beneath your limitations, beneath your doubts, beneath the unfinished parts of your journey, there is a treasure that cannot be measured. Great wealth has made its home within you. You do not need to earn it or create it. You need only become aware of it. Let that awareness fill you with gratitude and quiet wonder.
