## 27 - The Sacred Pause

> *Jesus said, "If you do not fast as regards the world, you will not find the kingdom. If you do not observe the Sabbath as a Sabbath, you will not see the father."*

This saying invites us into a different kind of fasting. Rather than speaking only of food, Jesus points toward our relationship with the world itself. We live surrounded by endless demands for our attention—news, opinions, ambitions, worries, distractions, and desires. Without realizing it, we can become so consumed by these things that we lose touch with the deeper life within us.

To "fast as regards the world" may be understood as creating space between ourselves and the forces that constantly pull at our awareness. It is not a rejection of life, but a release of our attachment to what is noisy, urgent, and fleeting. Just as fasting from food can sharpen the senses, fasting from constant distraction can awaken the soul.

Many people spend their lives searching for the kingdom while remaining perpetually occupied. The mind races from one concern to the next, leaving little room for silence. Yet the kingdom often reveals itself in stillness. It becomes visible when we stop grasping, stop striving, and become present to what is already here.

Jesus then speaks of the Sabbath. Beyond a particular day of rest, Sabbath can be understood as a way of being. It is the sacred art of ceasing. It is the willingness to step out of productivity, performance, and endless activity long enough to remember that our worth does not depend upon what we accomplish.

There is something within us that fears stopping. We worry that everything will fall apart if we are not constantly managing, planning, or producing. Yet Sabbath invites us to trust. It reminds us that the world continues turning without our effort and that divine life is already at work beneath the surface of things.

Perhaps this is why Jesus connects Sabbath with seeing the Father. When the noise settles and the striving quiets, our vision begins to clear. We notice beauty we once overlooked. We sense a presence we had forgotten. We discover that the sacred has been near all along, patiently waiting beneath the busyness of our lives.

Today, consider what it might mean to fast from the world for a few moments. Set aside the noise. Release the need to accomplish something. Enter a small Sabbath of the heart. In that quiet space, you may find that the kingdom is not far away. It has been waiting for your attention.
