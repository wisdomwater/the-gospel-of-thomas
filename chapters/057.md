## 57 - The Patient Gardener

> *Jesus said, "The kingdom of the father is like a man who had good seed. His enemy came by night and sowed weeds among the good seed. The man did not allow them to pull up the weeds; he said to them, 'I am afraid that you will go intending to pull up the weeds and pull up the wheat along with them.' For on the day of the harvest the weeds will be plainly visible, and they will be pulled up and burned."*

This parable begins with a mystery familiar to every human heart. Good seed is planted, yet weeds appear among it. We may wonder why a field intended for growth contains both beauty and disorder, both promise and complication. Why does life seem to hold such a mixture?

Often, when we notice the weeds within ourselves, our first impulse is to remove them immediately. We become impatient with our shortcomings, our fears, our habits, and our wounds. We want instant transformation. We want a perfectly clean field. Yet the gardener in this story responds with surprising restraint. He chooses patience over urgency.

Perhaps he understands something we often forget: growth is delicate. In our eagerness to eliminate what we dislike, we can damage what is still developing. Harsh self-judgment may uproot tenderness. Perfectionism may uproot joy. The desire to become flawless may unintentionally wound the very parts of us that are learning to flourish.

The Kingdom, then, may involve learning to live with unfinishedness. We are not asked to pretend the weeds are wheat, nor are we invited to ignore what needs healing. But we are encouraged to trust the process of growth. Over time, what is life-giving becomes clearer. What does not belong gradually reveals itself as well.

There is wisdom in allowing things to mature before drawing conclusions. Many experiences that once seemed like failures later reveal hidden gifts. Some inner struggles become the soil from which compassion grows. Some questions require seasons rather than answers. The patient gardener knows that clarity often arrives through time.

The image of harvest reminds us that discernment is part of spiritual growth. As awareness deepens, we begin to recognize what nourishes life and what diminishes it. Old patterns lose their hold. Fear gives way to trust. What is false naturally falls away, not because it is violently attacked, but because it no longer has a place in the light.

Today, be gentle with your field. Notice both the wheat and the weeds without becoming discouraged. Trust that growth is already happening beneath the surface. The Divine Gardener is not anxious about your journey. What is true within you is being nurtured, and in time, the harvest will reveal what love has been cultivating all along.

**Breath Prayer**

> Inhale: *Teach me patience*
>
> Exhale: *Trust the growing*
